1. Analysis of Operational Efficiency in a Contact Center

Objective

Data analysis project aimed at identifying underperforming agents in a contact center using a ranking system and a traffic-light classification based on performance metrics. The solution enabled the evaluation of both individual and team performance, supporting feedback processes, training decisions, and operational improvements.

Tools and Technologies

Python | Pandas | Seaborn |

processes developed

Data Cleaning | Data Transformation | Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) | Threshold and traffic light definition | Classification of operators by level

key questions

  • What is the main factor affecting contact center operational efficiency?
  • How are levels of operational inefficiency distributed, and what is their percentage impact?
  • What measures could be implemented to improve operational quality and efficiency?

Methodology

  • Data preprocessing: cleaning, integration, and standardization of operational data, checking for inconsistencies, duplicates, and missing values..
  • Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA): analysis of the distribution of variables, call types, and performance by operator to identify patterns of operational behavior and inefficiencies.
  • Business Analysis: Identification of critical factors related to missed calls and operational performance, enabling the generation of actionable insights.
  • Statistical hypothesis testing: verification of significant differences in the rate of missed calls for incoming and outgoing calls.

Featured Views

Ratio of the number of customers served to the total call duration per agent. The visualization includes a traffic-light system to identify critical agents and opportunities for improvement.

Correlation matrix between contact center operational variables, enabling the identification of relationships between call volume, call duration, missed calls, and levels of inefficiency.

Segmentation of operators using a traffic-light system based on operational performance indicators and risk levels.

Conclusions and Recommendations

  • The identified operational inefficiency exhibits a structural pattern primarily associated with a high rate of missed calls and the low effectiveness of outbound calls.
  • Missed calls showed a significant correlation with operational performance (r = 0.52), reaching critical levels when no operators were available.
  • Outgoing calls had a significantly higher drop rate than incoming calls (52.56% vs. 41.46%), a difference that was statistically significant (Z = 21.433, p < 0.001).

Recommended strategies

  • Optimizing databases and contact schedules to reduce missed calls and improve the effective contact rate.
  • Implementation of operational monitoring dashboards to track missed calls, call duration, and operator performance.
  • Strengthening training processes and efficiently managing call times to increase productivity and operational quality.

2. A/B Testing – Evaluate the effectiveness of a recommendation system in an online store

Objetivo

Evaluate whether the recently implemented recommendation system in the online store has led to a significant improvement in conversion rate by comparing the control and test groups.

Tools and Technologies

Python | Pandas | Seaborn | SciPy | Matplotlib|

Processes developed

Data Cleaning | Data Transformation | Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) | Conversion Funnel Building | A/B Testing | Results Evaluation |

Key Questions

  • Does the change implemented in Group B result in a significant improvement in the funnel?
  • Is it advisable to implement the change initially suggested for the online store ?
  • Does the statistical test confirm that the difference is significant?

Metodology

  • Data preprocessing: cleaning, integration, and standardization of operational data, checking for inconsistencies, duplicates, and missing values.
  • Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA): Comparison of sample sizes, distribution of events by user and by day. Definition of the eligible population and preliminary identification of potential anomalies.
  • Building the conversion funnel: At this stage, it was observed that Group A had more users than Group B at every stage, reflecting an initial imbalance between the groups—a factor to keep in mind when interpreting the results.
  • Running the A/B test: Although B shows a slight improvement in the product_page → product_cart stage, it loses ground in the final stage. The overall difference is not statistically significant.

Featured Views

The comparative funnel chart shows how users progress through each stage of the conversion process. It allows us to determine whether the new recommendation system led to improvements in engagement and purchase rates compared to the control group.

The daily events chart shows the trend in user activity over time during the experiment. It helps identify trends, spikes in engagement, and potential anomalies in the behavior of Groups A and B.

The summary chart provides an overview of users, conversions, and revenue for both groups. It facilitates direct comparison and shows that the control group outperformed the test group in terms of conversion rate and revenue.

Conclutions and Recomendations

  • Overall, Group B fails to outperform Group A in terms of funnel performance, showing only a slight improvement in the first stage and underperforming in the critical final stages.
  • The change implemented in B does not result in a significant improvement in the funnel. The observed difference is minimal (-1.1%) and does not add business value.
  • The statistical test confirms that the difference is not significant (p-value = 0.3024).

Recomendations

  • For the online store, we recommend NOT implementing the change until a new experiment with a better design has been conducted to correct the imbalances and biases that have been detected.

3. Analysis of a relational database on books

Objectives

Analyze a relational database containing information on books, publishers, authors, customer ratings, and book reviews to develop a value proposition focused on a new product.

Tools and Technologies

SQL |SQLAlchemy | Python | Pandas | Seaborn | SciPy | Matplotlib|

Developed processes

Database Exploration | Reviewing Tables and Relationships | Various Queries | Analyzing Results |

Key questions

  • How can the platform strike a balance between editorial concentration and promoting the diversity and visibility of smaller publishers?
  • What motivational strategies could encourage the most active users to write more high-quality reviews, in addition to rating books?
  • How does the recognition of established authors influence the perception of quality, and how can that influence be leveraged to increase the visibility of lesser-known authors?

Metodology

  • Database exploration: Identification of tables, primary and foreign keys, and review of relationships between authors, books, publishers, ratings, and reviews. Display of the first few rows using dynamic queries in Python and Pandas.
  • Writing SQL Queries: Building queries using aggregate functions (COUNT, AVG), filters (WHERE, HAVING), and joins (INNER JOIN) to meet the following objectives: number of books published, reviews, average ratings, most prolific publishers, and top-rated authors.
  • Analysis of Results: Interpretation of findings regarding user engagement, variation in ratings, editorial concentration, and the influence of well-known authors. Identification of key patterns in user interaction and editorial output.
  • Conclusions and Recommendations: Developing strategies to encourage reviews through gamification, balancing editorial focus with personalized recommendations, and leveraging the influence of well-known authors to showcase new talent.

Featured Views

This chart shows the distribution of the number of reviews written by users. Most users contribute between 15 and 20 reviews, indicating a moderately active group, while a few users write very few or a large number of reviews. This reinforces the conclusion that active participation requires greater commitment.

The heat map reveals the relationship between the number of reviews and the average rating of the books. It shows that most titles with good ratings (3.8–4.6) have few reviews, suggesting that the perception of quality is not always accompanied by high review engagement.

The bubble plot incorporates three dimensions: number of books published, average rating, and popularity as measured by reviews. It illustrates how well-known authors combine productivity and quality with a higher volume of reviews, while others show a lower profile or greater variability in ratings.

Conclusions and Recommendations

  • Few publishers, such as Penguin Books and Vintage, dominate book publishing, which points to a concentrated market structure.
  • The most engaged users, who have rated more than 50 books, write an average of 24 reviews, confirming that creating written content requires greater effort and motivation.
  • The reading experience depends heavily on the author, the content, and readers' individual expectations; ratings vary widely, with some books receiving top scores of 5.0 while others receive significantly lower ratings.

Recomendations

  • Encourage users to write reviews by implementing incentives such as gamification, recognition, or badges to motivate them to do so.
  • Manage variations in perceptions of quality using filtering and segmentation tools (by genre, style, and popularity) that allow users to find books that better match their interests and tastes.
